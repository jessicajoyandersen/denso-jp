て-imasu — one more use
You learned て-imasu as ongoing action. But some verbs use it for a resultant state — the action is complete, and the result is still true now.

The time word is the clue:

（今） tabete imasu. → I am eating right now. ongoing action
（今）食べています。
（もう） tabete imasu. → I have already eaten. resultant state
（もう）食べています。
Same て-imasu, different meaning — the context tells you which one.

💡 Last session, some of you chose kite imasu for "a supervisor asks if someone is at Station 3." That was actually correct — you just learned it before it was planned. Kite imasu = they have come and are there now as a result. Well done.
Kite imasu. → They have come and are here now. (resultant state — arrival complete)
Suwatte imasu. → I am sitting. (result of having sat down)
Shitte imasu. → I know. (result of having learned)

Block B
Saying Where Things Are
You already know に for destination. Now: に for location. Same particle — this time with います or あります instead of a movement verb.
います
imasu
People and animals — living, moving things
Tanaka-san が います
Supervisor が います
Neko が います
あります
arimasu
Objects and things — non-living items
Tejun が あります
Kikai が あります
Repōto が あります
The full pattern
[person/thing] が [place] に います/あります

が marks what is there. に marks where. います/あります says it exists there.

Tanaka-san ga Station 3 ni imasu.
Tanaka-san is at Station 3.

Tejun ga tsukue no ue ni arimasu.
The procedure sheet is on the desk.
が here vs は in other sentences
When you use が with います/あります, you are pointing out specific, new information — "it is this person who is there." This is the same が from S8: が for focus, は for topic. Both are correct in different contexts — が is the natural choice when location is new information.
Quick Check — います or あります?
Floor Exchanges

I-adjectives end in い and attach directly to desu. One rule covers all of them.

[i-adjective] + desu — for statements
[i-adjective] + [noun] — to describe a noun directly

Nemui desu. → I'm sleepy.
Hayai kikai desu. → It's a fast machine.

Block D
kara — Giving a Reason
kara connects a reason to a result. The reason comes first, then kara, then what you're going to do.
[reason] kara, [result].
Nemui desu kara, nemasu. → I'm sleepy, so I'm going to sleep.
Muzukashii desu kara, mō ichi-do hanashite kudasai. → It's difficult, so please say it again.
Kikai ga furui desu kara, chekku shite kudasai. → The machine is old, so please check it.
How it feels in English
Think of kara as "so" or "because" — it works both ways in Japanese.

"Nemui desu kara, nemasu" = "I'm sleepy, so I'll sleep" or "Because I'm sleepy, I'll sleep."

The reason always comes first. You're setting up the "why" before you say what happens.

Block E
Putting It All Together
These exchanges combine location に, adjectives, and kara in natural floor conversations.
A worker explains why she needs help with the procedure.
Worker
Kono tejun wa muzukashii desu kara, oshiete kudasai.
この手順は難しいですから、教えてください。
This procedure is difficult, so please teach me.
Senior
Wakarimashita. Issho ni mimashō.
Understood. Let's look at it together.
💡 i-adjective + desu + kara = reason. oshiete = te-form of oshieru (RU-verb, to teach).
You're reporting to your supervisor that the machine needs attention.
Worker
Kono kikai wa furui desu kara, chekku shite kudasai. Kikai ga Station 3 ni arimasu.
この機械は古いですから、チェックしてください。機械がステーション3にあります。
This machine is old, so please check it. The machine is at Station 3.
💡 kara reason + location に あります combined. furui = old (i-adjective).
End of shift — you tell your supervisor you're tired and going home.
Worker
Tsukaremashita. Nemui desu kara, kaette mo ii desu ka?
疲れました。眠いですから、帰ってもいいですか？
I'm tired. I'm sleepy, so is it okay if I go home?
Supervisor
Hai, otsukaresama deshita.
Yes, good work today.
💡 "kaette mo ii desu ka" = "may I go home?" — preview only, not tested. kara gives the reason before asking.
