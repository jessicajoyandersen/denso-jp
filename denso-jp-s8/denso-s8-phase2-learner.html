を — The Object Particle
Mark what's being acted on. One particle, any action verb.
The Pattern
[ Subject ] は  [ Object ] を  [ Verb ]
を (pronounced wo or just o) marks the direct object — the thing being acted on by the verb.

Watashi wa kono kikai wo tsukaimasu. — I use this machine.
Tejun wo mite kudasai. — Please check the procedure.
⚡ S6 Connection — You Already Used を
In Session 6 you heard: Ano kikai wo tsukatte kudasai — please use that machine over there. The を was already there, doing its job. Today you just learn its name and how to use it yourself.
Verb Pairs — -masu form and -te kudasai

💡 Note — Fixed Pairs
The -masu and -te kudasai forms are presented as fixed pairs — don't worry about how the ending changes for now. Learn them as chunks: tsukaimasu / tsukatte kudasai, mimasu / mite kudasai. The pattern will become natural with use.

Block C
は vs が — The Core Distinction
You've used both. Now you'll understand why.
Why This Matters
は and が are the two particles learners mix up most — and for good reason. They can often appear in the same position in a sentence, but they say different things. Getting this right isn't just grammar — it changes the nuance of what you're communicating.
Part 1 — は marks the Topic
は — "As for X…"
は (wa) marks the topic of the sentence — what you're talking about. Think of it as "as for X" or "speaking of X." It sets the frame.

Watashi wa opereetaa desu. — As for me, I'm an operator.
Kono kikai wa furui desu. — As for this machine, it's old.
Yamada-san wa A-rain desu. — As for Yamada-san, he's on A-line.

You've been using は since Session 5 with X は Y です. That was always a topic frame: "as for X, it is Y."
Part 2 — が marks the Subject / Focus
が — "X is the one who…"
が (ga) marks the grammatical subject, and often carries focus — it identifies who or what specifically. Think of it as putting a spotlight on something.

Dare ga kono tejun wo yomimashita ka? — Who read this procedure?
Yamada-san ga yomimashita. — Yamada-san did. (が identifies the answer)

Kono kikai ga furui desu. — This machine is the old one. (identifying which)

You've also been using が since Session 7: Futtobooru が suki desu. が spotlights what's liked — "as for what I like, it's football."
Part 3 — Side by Side
The Key Contrast
は version が version
Tanaka-san wa kimashita.
As for Tanaka-san, he came.
(topic — others may or may not have)		 Tanaka-san ga kimashita.
Tanaka-san is the one who came.
(identifies who — answering "dare ga?")
Kono kikai wa furui desu.
As for this machine, it's old.
(stating a fact about the topic)		 Kono kikai ga furui desu.
This machine is the old one.
(identifying which machine is old)
Watashi wa wakarimasu.
As for me, I understand.
(may imply: others don't)		 Watashi ga wakarimasu.
I'm the one who understands.
(strong identification)
Part 4 — Contrastive は
💡 は Can Imply "But Not Others"
は has a subtle contrastive flavour — it can imply contrast with something unstated:

Kono tejun wa yomimashita. — I read this procedure. (implying: maybe not the others)
Yamada-san wa kimashita. — Yamada-san came. (implying: others maybe didn't)

が doesn't carry this implication. が just identifies. So if you want to state something neutrally about a subject, が is often the cleaner choice. If you want to set a topic or imply contrast, は.
Part 5 — Fixed Patterns to Lock In
Patterns You Already Know
These patterns are fixed — don't swap the particles:

X は Y です (S5) — always は. Topic frame: "as for X, it is Y."
[thing] が 好きです (S7) — always が. Spotlights what's liked.
Dare が ~? — always が. が answers "who/what" questions.

When you're unsure: if you're setting the topic ("speaking of X"), use は. If you're identifying who or what does something, use が.
