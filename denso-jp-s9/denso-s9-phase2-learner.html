Block B
Why Verbs Change
Japanese verbs belong to one of three groups. The group determines how the verb ending changes when you conjugate it. Today you're learning two of the three.
The Key Idea
You've been using conjugated forms since Session 6 — mite kudasai, oshite kudasai, tsukatte kudasai. Those were presented as fixed chunks. Today you learn why the ending changes — and how to do it yourself with any verb in the same group.
The Three Groups
Group 1
U-Verbs
The largest group. Multiple sub-patterns depending on the final consonant. kaku, nomu, hanasu, etc.
Several sub-patterns
→ Coming in Session 10
Group 2
RU-Verbs
Dictionary form ends in -eru or -iru. Conjugation is dead simple — one rule only.
Drop る, add て
✓ Today's focus
Group 3
Irregulars
Only two verbs. Memorise them. They don't follow any pattern.
Just 2 verbs
✓ Today's focus
💡 Honest Note on Group 1
Group 1 (U-verbs) has several sub-patterns based on the verb's final consonant — kaku → kaite, nomu → nonde, hanasu → hanashite. It's the biggest group, and it deserves its own session. You'll learn it in S10. For now: Group 2 and the irregulars get you a long way on the floor.
What is the て-form?
The て-form — Two Uses You'll Use Every Day
The て-form is the base for several important patterns. Today you need two:

1. [Verb-て] kudasai — "Please [verb]." The request form you've been using since S6.
2. [Verb-て] imasu — "[Someone] is [verb]-ing." Ongoing action. (Preview only — S10/S11 will drill this.)

Everything starts with getting the て-form right.

Block C
Group 2 — One Rule Only
RU-verbs are conjugated by dropping the final る and adding て. That's the entire rule.
Drop る  +  add て
Example: miru (to look) → drop る → mi → add て → mite
⚡ The Unlock Moment
You used mite kudasai in Session 8. Now you know why it's mite: miru → drop る → mi → add て → mite. The chunk you learned is the て-form of miru + kudasai. Every Group 2 verb works the same way.

💡 ikimasu — a surprise
You might expect ikimasu (to go) to be irregular too — it sounds similar to kimasu. It's not. Ikimasu is Group 1, and its て-form is itte (not iite). You'll see itte in Block E when you use it with に. For now just flag it — kite ≠ itte.

📍 Block E — に (Destination Particle)
Waiting

Block E
に — Where You're Going
The particle に marks the destination of movement. "Go to Station 3." "Come here." "Take it over there." One particle, any movement verb.
The Pattern
[Destination] に [movement verb-て] kudasai
Station 3 ni itte kudasai. — Please go to Station 3.
Koko ni kite kudasai. — Please come here.

Notice: itte (go) and kite (come) are both て-forms — the same forms you just learned in Blocks C and D, now used with a destination marked by に.
⚡ itte — ikimasu's te-form
Ikimasu (to go) is Group 1, but its て-form is itte — not ikite. This is one of the Group 1 patterns where く → いて (k-sounds shift). You don't need to know why yet — just lock in: ikimasu → itte. You'll see this pattern again in S10 with all Group 1 verbs.

Block F
Putting It Together
Full exchanges combining て-form requests, に destination, and everything from S1–S8. Say the lines out loud with a partner.
A Japanese line leader needs to direct a worker to a different station.
Line Leader
Station ni ni itte kudasai.
ステーション2に行ってください。
Please go to Station 2.
Worker
Hai, wakarimashita.
はい、わかりました。
Yes, understood.
💡 Pattern: [destination] に itte kudasai. に marks where they're going. Itte = て-form of ikimasu.
A supervisor calls a worker over from across the floor.
Supervisor
Chotto kite kudasai.
ちょっと来てください。
Please come here a moment.
Worker
Hai, tadaima.
はい、ただいま。
Yes, right away. (lit. "I'm here now")
💡 Hai, tadaima = yes, right away / coming. Standard floor response to being called over. Kite = て-form of kimasu (irregular Group 3).
A line leader sends a worker to check a procedure at another station, combining direction and a task.
Line Leader
Station san ni itte, kono tejun wo mite kudasai.
ステーション3に行って、この手順を見てください。
Please go to Station 3 and check this procedure.
Worker
Hai, wakarimashita. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.
はい、わかりました。よろしくお願いします。
Yes, understood. I'll take care of it.
💡 Two-action sentence: itte (go) + mite kudasai (check). に marks the destination; を marks the procedure as the object. Both て-forms in one sentence.
A quality inspector asks a worker to open the safety cover and do a visual check.
Inspector
Kono kabaa wo akete, kakunin shite kudasai.
このカバーを開けて、確認してください。
Please open this cover and do a check.
Worker
Hai, wakarimashita.
はい、わかりました。
Yes, understood.
💡 Akete = Group 2 て-form of akeru. Shite kudasai = Group 3 irregular. Two consecutive て-form requests chained with a comma pause.
